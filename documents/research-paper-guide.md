## The Complete Guide to Writing an A-Grade Research Paper

This guide walks you through every stage of writing a research paper. It is designed for students at any level who want to produce work that is clear, well-argued, and properly supported by evidence.

### 1. Understanding the Assignment

Before you write a single word, make sure you understand what is being asked.

**Read the brief carefully.** Identify: the topic or question, the word count, the submission deadline, the referencing style required, the assessment criteria, and any specific structural requirements. If anything is unclear, ask your lecturer — this is not a sign of weakness; it is a sign of diligence.

**Identify the task verbs.** Academic assignments use specific verbs that signal what kind of thinking is expected:
- **Analyse:** Break something into its component parts and examine how they relate
- **Evaluate:** Make a judgement based on evidence, weighing strengths and weaknesses
- **Discuss:** Explore different perspectives on an issue, presenting arguments and counterarguments
- **Compare/Contrast:** Identify similarities and differences between two or more things
- **Critically assess:** Evaluate with particular attention to limitations and assumptions

**Know the marking criteria.** Most assignments come with a rubric or marking guide. Read it before you start writing, not after. Understand what percentage of marks goes to argument quality, evidence use, structure, and presentation.

### 2. Choosing and Narrowing a Topic

A good research question is specific, arguable, and researchable.

**Start broad, then narrow.** Begin with a general area of interest, then progressively focus until you have a question that can be answered within your word count. A 2,000-word essay cannot cover "the causes of climate change." It can cover "the role of agricultural subsidies in driving deforestation in the Brazilian Amazon between 2010 and 2020."

**Test your question.** A strong research question passes three tests:
1. **The 'so what' test:** Does answering this question matter? Does it contribute to understanding?
2. **The 'arguability' test:** Could a reasonable, informed person disagree with your answer?
3. **The 'feasibility' test:** Can you find enough sources to support an argument within the available time?

### 3. Finding and Evaluating Sources

Your paper is only as strong as its evidence base.

**Where to search:** Start with your university library's database search tools. Google Scholar is useful for discovery but always access the full text through your library. Key databases by discipline include: JSTOR (humanities), PubMed (health sciences), IEEE Xplore (engineering), Web of Science (cross-disciplinary), and PsycINFO (psychology).

**How many sources:** As a rough guide, aim for one to two sources per 200 words of your paper. A 3,000-word essay should engage with 15-30 sources. Quality matters more than quantity — ten well-chosen, carefully analysed sources are better than thirty superficially cited ones.

**Evaluating sources:** For each source, consider:
- **Authority:** Who wrote it? What are their credentials? Where was it published?
- **Currency:** When was it published? Is it still relevant?
- **Relevance:** Does it directly address your research question?
- **Methodology:** (For empirical studies) Is the research design sound? Is the sample size adequate?
- **Bias:** Does the author have a vested interest? Are alternative viewpoints acknowledged?

### 4. Building Your Argument

A research paper is not a report — it is an argument.

**Craft a thesis statement.** Your thesis is the central claim of your paper. It should be: specific (not vague or general), contestable (not a statement of fact), and supported (you have evidence for it).

**Structure your argument.** Each body section should present one supporting claim. Together, these claims build the case for your thesis. Arrange them in a logical order — from foundational to complex, from least to most controversial, or from cause to effect.

**Address counterarguments.** Strong papers anticipate objections. For each major claim, consider what a critic might say, and address it. This shows intellectual maturity and strengthens your argument by demonstrating you have considered alternative perspectives.

### 5. Writing the First Draft

Get words on the page. Perfectionism at the drafting stage is the enemy of productivity.

**Do not start with the introduction.** Write the body sections first, where your argument lives. Once you know what you have argued, writing the introduction and conclusion becomes straightforward.

**One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph should have: a topic sentence stating the paragraph's main point, evidence supporting that point (with citations), your analysis of what the evidence means, and a transition to the next paragraph.

**Integrate citations, do not stack them.** Bad: "Smith (2020) says X. Jones (2021) says Y. Brown (2022) says Z." Good: "While Smith (2020) established that X, subsequent research by Jones (2021) challenged this finding, demonstrating Y. Brown (2022) reconciled these perspectives by showing Z."

### 6. Revising and Polishing

Good writing is rewriting. Plan to revise at least twice.

**First revision — structure and argument.** Read the whole paper and ask: Does the argument hold together? Does each section contribute to the thesis? Are there gaps in the logic? Are counterarguments addressed? Is the evidence sufficient?

**Second revision — clarity and style.** Read sentence by sentence. Is each sentence clear? Is the academic register consistent? Are transitions smooth? Is there unnecessary repetition? Are sentences varied in length and structure?

**Final check — technical accuracy.** Verify: citation formatting is consistent, the reference list matches in-text citations, spelling and grammar are correct, headings and formatting follow the required style, and the word count is within the specified range.

### 7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Descriptive rather than analytical writing.** Do not just describe what sources say — analyse what their findings mean for your argument.
- **Overciting.** Not every sentence needs a citation. Your own analytical claims do not require one — only factual statements and ideas from other sources do.
- **Conclusion introduces new material.** The conclusion synthesises existing arguments. It does not introduce new evidence or new lines of argument.
- **Ignoring the word count.** Being significantly over or under the word count signals poor planning. If you are over, you have likely tried to cover too much. If you are under, you have not developed your arguments sufficiently.
- **Last-minute writing.** Research papers cannot be written well the night before the deadline. Plan backwards from the submission date: one week for polishing, one week for drafting, one week for research and planning.
