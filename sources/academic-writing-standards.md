## Academic Writing Standards by Discipline

This source provides a reference guide to the conventions that govern academic writing across major disciplinary families. Use it to calibrate the tone, structure, and formatting of research papers.

### Sciences (Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Environmental Science)

**Structure:** IMRaD (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion). Some fields accept variations such as a combined Results and Discussion section.
**Voice:** Third person, predominantly passive ("The samples were analysed..."). First person is increasingly accepted in some journals but remains uncommon in student work.
**Citation style:** Typically Vancouver (numbered) or APA (author-date), depending on the institution.
**Hedging conventions:** Strong hedging expected for novel findings ("These results suggest..." rather than "This proves..."). Quantitative hedging is common ("approximately," "a significant proportion").
**Paragraph length:** Short to medium (80-150 words). Dense, concise paragraphs are preferred.
**Key conventions:** Precise methodology descriptions, statistical reporting (p-values, confidence intervals), figure and table integration, consistent use of SI units.

### Social Sciences (Psychology, Sociology, Political Science, Education)

**Structure:** Varies by sub-discipline. Psychology follows IMRaD closely. Sociology and political science use thematic essay structures. Education often blends empirical and theoretical approaches.
**Voice:** Third person is standard. First person is acceptable for reflexive or qualitative research ("I conducted semi-structured interviews...").
**Citation style:** APA 7th edition is dominant. Some institutions use Harvard.
**Hedging conventions:** Moderate hedging. Social scientists balance caution with clarity. Avoid over-hedging to the point of meaninglessness.
**Paragraph length:** Medium (120-200 words). Adequate development expected.
**Key conventions:** Theoretical framing in introduction, operationalisation of key concepts, ethical considerations for research involving participants, clear distinction between findings and interpretation.

### Humanities (English Literature, History, Philosophy, Classics)

**Structure:** Essay format — introduction with thesis, body paragraphs organised by argument, conclusion. No methods or results sections.
**Voice:** First person is widely accepted and often expected ("I argue that..."). The author's interpretive stance is central.
**Citation style:** Chicago (notes-bibliography) or MLA, depending on the discipline. History uses Chicago. Literary studies use MLA or Chicago.
**Hedging conventions:** More assertive argumentation is expected. Excessive hedging is seen as weak. "I argue" is preferred to "It could be suggested."
**Paragraph length:** Long (150-300 words). Extensive development of ideas within a single paragraph is expected.
**Key conventions:** Close textual analysis, extensive engagement with primary sources, footnotes for discursive asides, lengthy literature engagement, historiographical awareness (History).

### Law

**Structure:** Problem-question format (IRAC: Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) for problem questions. Essay format for discursive questions.
**Voice:** Third person. Impersonal and authoritative.
**Citation style:** OSCOLA (UK), Bluebook (US), or AGLC (Australia). Footnotes, not in-text citations.
**Hedging conventions:** Minimal. Legal writing demands precision and authority. "It is submitted that..." is the standard hedge.
**Paragraph length:** Varies. Shorter paragraphs for case analysis, longer for theoretical discussion.
**Key conventions:** Accurate case citation, distinction between ratio and obiter, statutory interpretation, precedent-based reasoning.

### Engineering and Computer Science

**Structure:** Technical report format — Introduction, Background, Methodology/Design, Implementation, Results/Evaluation, Conclusion.
**Voice:** Third person, passive voice dominant. Impersonal and technical.
**Citation style:** IEEE (numbered) is standard.
**Hedging conventions:** Minimal hedging. Technical claims are supported by data, not qualified by language.
**Paragraph length:** Short (60-120 words). Bullet points and numbered lists are acceptable.
**Key conventions:** Specification-focused writing, algorithm descriptions, performance metrics, system diagrams, reproducibility emphasis.

### Business and Management

**Structure:** Report format with executive summary, or essay format depending on the assignment.
**Voice:** Third person for academic work. First person acceptable for reflective assignments.
**Citation style:** Harvard or APA.
**Hedging conventions:** Moderate. Claims about market behaviour and organisational outcomes require evidential support.
**Paragraph length:** Medium (100-180 words).
**Key conventions:** Framework application (SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, etc.), data-driven analysis, practical recommendations, industry examples.

### Common Standards Across All Disciplines

- **Academic integrity:** All ideas from other sources must be attributed. Paraphrase is preferred over direct quotation except where the exact wording matters.
- **Paragraph structure:** Topic sentence → development → evidence → analysis → link to next point.
- **Introduction conventions:** Context → narrowing → research question/thesis → paper overview.
- **Conclusion conventions:** Restate thesis (reworded) → synthesise arguments → acknowledge limitations → future directions → closing statement.
- **Formatting:** Consistent heading hierarchy, 12pt readable font, appropriate line spacing, page numbers, title page where required.
